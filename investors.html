The thesis

Health has never had a definition you could put a number to.

Every diagnostic on the market measures deviation from a population average. None of them start from a first-principles statement of what health is. That gap is where Fieldflux started.

We wrote the definition as formal mathematics, proved it — machine-checked — into the observable physics, and ported it into a runtime that reads a person's whole physiology in real time. What protects it is not a feature set but a body of foundational science and a measurement architecture — years of work that would have to be reproduced from first principles.

Defensibility

Three layers, stronger together.

Foundational science, protected IP, and a product in the market — each one hard to copy, and stronger together.

01 · The science

A formal ontology of health

A machine-checked mathematical definition of health — built over years, not a dataset or a heuristic. The genuinely hard part came before the mathematics: to formalize health, test it, and build instruments that measure it, someone first has to know what it actually is — not only human physical health, but health as such, across scale, from bird to bee, flower to tree, planet to star. That understanding is the category-defining asset — the deepest reason this is hard to reproduce, though far from the only one.

02 · The IP

A patent-pending instrument

QPCI — Quantum Phase-Coherence Imaging — covers a measurement architecture that runs identically from an iPhone to a hospital array. Filed as a non-provisional in June 2026, on a June 2025 priority date, with 133 independent and dependent claims.

03 · The product

A live consumer app

Membrane Health is in the market on Apple Watch — proof the science runs on hardware people already own, with a clear path from consumer to professional to clinical.

Risk posture

Measurement, not diagnosis — on purpose.

We form images, maps, and fields of the body's response. We do not output diagnoses or treatment decisions.

That discipline is a strategic choice as much as an ethical one: it keeps the near-term product in a defensible regulatory position while the clinical instruments mature, and it keeps every claim we make honest and auditable. Our standards →
